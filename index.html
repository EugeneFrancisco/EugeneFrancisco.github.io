Hello, I'm a second year math student at Stanford. My primary interests at the moment are in stochastic processes, reinforcement learning, and (seperately) spectral graph theory.

I code a lot in my free time mostly because I think the best way to learn something is to code it up. In fact my journey into math began with programming Python sampling simulations of different distributions in AP Stats during lockdown.

On the math side, I love abstract algebra and graph theory. In fact, my favorite class at Stanford was a ring theory class I took in my freshman year (Math 62DM); you can find a project in this in my Projects section.

At Stanford, I am an undergraduate TA for the CS198 program, where I teach a weekly discussion section for the introductory programming classes. I also write combinatorics and probability problems for the Stanford Math Tournament.

You can find my resume here.

Deep Q Learning for Option Pricing:
I implemented a deep Q learning algorithm to learn fair option prices under fat tailed market conditions by treating option pricing as an MDP problem. I was really interested in how viable it was to learn option prices using reinforcement learning; in the end, I realized that this kind of learning requires lots of compute, especially in creating simulation traces for the Q learner.

Visualizing the Ito Correction:
In a probability class I took last year (Math 63DM), I was introduced to Brownian Motion and stochastic calculus. Over the summer, interested in its applications to financial derivatives pricing, I learned some more SDEs and wrote visualizations to see the effect of Ito's correction term.

Finding primitive roots of Fq:
I made this to find generators - and more generally (pun intended) the order of different elements - in finite fields of size \(p^2\).

Tournaments Talk:
Slides for a talk on the Quadratic Residue Tournaments, from Alon & Spencer's The Probabilistic Method, a clever tournament construction which leads to very poor ranking of the players.
